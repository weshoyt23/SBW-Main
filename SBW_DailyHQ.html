Content Philosophy — Non-Negotiable
Content Direction — Locked In
WE ARE NOT OPTIMIZING FOR VIRAL.
WE ARE OPTIMIZING FOR TRUST.
WE ARE NOT BUILDING A BRAND.
WE ARE BUILDING A COMMUNITY.

The clothing is the vehicle. The Collective is the destination. Content always points toward the community.

Every piece of content SBW puts out is measured against one question: does this build trust and direct people toward the community? Not does it perform. Not does it go viral. Does it make someone want to be part of what we're building.

Primary Content Focus (~70%)

The Discord. The Collective. Creativity. Community. Art. Sharing ideas. Coming together.

Most SBW content is about the community — what happens inside the Discord, what members are making, how people are finding themselves through creative expression, what it feels like to be in a space where art is the only requirement for entry.

🎨
Member art spotlights — sharing what Collective members are making. Celebrating their work publicly.
💬
Discord conversations — sharing real moments of creative collaboration, critique, and community from inside the channel.
✦
Creative prompts and challenges — content that invites the audience to make something. Directing them toward participation, not passive watching.
🤝
The collective story — who's in it, what they make, how they got in, what it means to them. The community is the content.
🚪
The invitation — content that makes people want in. That creates the feeling of missing something real. The door is there. Here's what's on the other side.
Secondary Content (~20%)

The founder story. Building SBW in public. The real process — Uber Eats runs, design decisions, hard days, wins. Transparency about what it actually takes. This builds trust with people who aren't in the Collective yet and makes them want in.

📱
BTS of building the brand — real, unglamorous, honest
🎯
Design process — how pieces are made, what decisions look like
📖
The story of SBW — why it exists, what it's trying to prove
Product Content (~10%) — The Smallest Slice

Drop announcements, product shots, launch content. The smallest portion of content — but when it lands, it lands on an audience that already trusts the brand and wants in. The community content builds the demand. The product content converts it.

The Core Insight

Most clothing brands make content about the clothes. SBW makes content about the community the clothes get you into. The product becomes proof of membership — not the point of the content.

Every post should make someone feel one of two things: "I want to be part of that" or "I already am part of that." Nothing else matters.

Viral views are borrowed. Trust is owned. Community compounds. We are building something that compounds.

Content Influences — Study These Two
Foundational Reference — Systems & Content
DAN MARTELL
danmartell.com · Buy Back Your Time · SaaS Academy

Author of Buy Back Your Time. Three successful software exits. Angel investor in 50+ companies. Known for systems thinking, time management, and building businesses that run without the founder burning out.

CAPTURE DON'T CREATE
Don't manufacture content from scratch. Document what you're already doing. Talk through your process as you do it. The nuance comes through naturally. Every Uber Eats shift, every design decision, every milestone — that's content waiting to be captured.
SYSTEMS OVER HUSTLE
Build content systems that don't require performing creativity on demand. The goal is a content operation that runs consistently even when life is hard. Consistency beats inspiration.
CREATE FROM FLOW
His philosophy: create from a place of flow, remove what doesn't serve you, and scale impact through systems — not burnout. Sound familiar? That's the SBW ethos applied to content.
Foundational Reference — Brand & Content Strategy
CALEB RALSTON
calebralston.com · Worked with Gary Vee, Alex & Leila Hormozi

Brand strategist and content producer with 16 years experience. Has helped generate billions of organic impressions. Now teaches founders how to build magnetic brands and real media machines that scale — sustainably and honestly.

TEACH FROM PROCESS
"Teach from the process, not the persona. If you are in the arena, you will never run out of content ideas." Wesley is in the arena. Building SBW live, in public, with real stakes. That IS the content.
DEPTH OVER DIZZINESS
Build one content pillar at a time. Create a cadence you can keep when life gets messy. Sustainability beats volume. A cadence you can hold for two years beats a sprint you hold for two weeks.
DON'T NICHE DOWN
Niching down leads to creator burnout and audience fatigue. SBW is not "a streetwear brand that posts about streetwear." It's a founder with a full life, a real mission, and a community — and all of that is the content.
BUILD TRUST WITH DATA
Use case studies and real results to build audience trust. Document the journey with numbers, not just feelings. When Drop 1 sells, show it. When it doesn't, explain why. That transparency is the brand.
How They Apply to SBW Together

Dan Martell gives SBW the systems — how to build a content operation that runs without burning out. Caleb Ralston gives SBW the strategy — what to stand for, what to say no to, how to build something people trust over years. Neither of them optimize for viral. Both of them optimize for compounding trust. That's the SBW model.

Tips & Tricks for Young Founders
Money
Pay yourself in owner's draws, not salary. As an LLC you take owner's draws from profit. Don't set a salary you can't sustain. Take what the business can afford each month and leave the rest in Mercury.
Money
Never spend money you haven't earned yet. Projected revenue is not revenue. Only make purchases from money already in Mercury — not money you expect to make.
Mindset
Document everything from day one. Screenshots, receipts, decisions, results. Future you needs this for taxes, investors, portfolio, and the moments when you forget how far you've come.
Mindset
Done beats perfect — always. A post published at 80% is infinitely more valuable than a post perfected but never posted. The market will tell you what needs to be better. Your own standards will keep you invisible.
Legal
Get the LLC before you make a single sale. One lawsuit before you're incorporated and your personal assets are exposed. The $300 is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.
Legal
Get everything in writing. Supplier agreements, freelance contracts, any handshake deal. A text message chain is not a contract. AND.CO has free templates for freelancers.
Growth
Your first 100 customers are your marketing team. Treat them like gold. Personal DMs, handwritten notes, early access. They will tell people. Word of mouth from true believers outperforms paid ads at your stage every time.
Growth
Say no to things that don't fit the brand. Custom orders, collabs, discounts, product ideas that pull SBW off-brand — every yes to the wrong thing is a no to the right thing. Brand clarity is your moat.
AI & Tools
Use AI to move faster, not to replace your voice. Let AI handle research, structure, and repetitive work. Never let it replace the thing that makes SBW SBW — your specific creative point of view.
AI & Tools
Build your toolkit before you need it. Klaviyo before your first email subscriber. Wave before your first transaction. Judge.me before your first review. Set it up now so it runs automatically when the moment comes.
Wellbeing
Comparison is the enemy of momentum. Other brands' highlight reels are not their full story. You're seeing their Month 18 while living your Month 1. The only valid comparison is you yesterday vs you today.
Wellbeing
Find your people. One other founder you can be honest with is worth more than a thousand followers. SCORE, Capital Factory events, Austin Creative Alliance — show up. The network compounds just like the business does.

Discord Agent — Master Prompt Foundation
What This Agent Actually Is

Not a bot dropping generic quotes. A community curator that understands SBW's voice, knows the members, tracks active conversations, and makes purposeful interventions that feel human. The difference between a bot that posts "Here's your daily inspiration 🌟" and an agent that says "This image Wesley posted last week reminded me of something — what would you make if you had to respond to it visually?" is everything.

Core Behaviors
🎨
Weekly creative prompts — tailored to SBW's aesthetic world. Grounded in Austin, the design philosophy, the themes of whatever drop is coming. Not generic art prompts.
🔥
Drop countdown activations — builds anticipation in Discord before it hits Instagram. Members feel like insiders because they are.
🤝
Collaboration seeding — notices two members who've posted similar work and connects them. Transforms a chat into a creative collective.
📂
Archive highlights — surfaces submission archive pieces every few days with framing questions. Keeps founding members' work visible and valued.
🖼️
Mood board threads — drops a visual direction or reference, asks members to respond with their own. Crowdsourced creative research happening organically.
The Division of Labor
Agent Handles
Consistency. Routine prompts. Activation between drops. Archive highlights. Collaboration connections.
Wesley Handles
Meaning. Responding to breakthrough pieces. Design decisions with the community. Showing up when it matters.
Voice Rules — Non-Negotiable

The agent must sound indistinguishable from something Wesley would actually say. Not corporate. Not generic inspirational. Not try-hard. The same tone that lives in the invitation page, the tag copy, Wesley's captions. Every message the agent sends should pass the test: "Would Wesley actually say this?" If the answer is no — the agent doesn't say it.

Character Concept — Long Game
THE RODEO CLOWNS

The concept: SBW's characters are dressed like rodeo clowns. Wacky, wild, unpredictable on the surface — but their entire purpose is to protect you and pick you up when you're down. That's the metaphor. That's the brand's spirit.

Rodeo clowns take the hits so the rider doesn't have to. They're in the arena when everyone else runs. They look absurd from the outside but they're the most important person in the room when things go wrong. That's SBW's Collective in one image.

Character Design Concept — Logged
ANIMATED CHARACTERS IN REAL LOCATIONS

Wesley already does street photography — capturing real Austin locations, textures, walls, alleys, parks, architecture. The concept: use those real-world photos as backgrounds and composite the three SBW animated characters into them. Cartoony, illustrated characters existing inside real Austin photographs.

📸
Real backgrounds: Wesley's street photography — Austin walls, spots, textures, architecture, parks, alleyways. Shot on location, authentic, unglamorous.
🎨
Animated characters composited in: The three SBW characters — cartoony, illustrated — placed into those real photographs. Living in real Austin but drawn, not photographed.
👕
On the shirts: This mixed-reality aesthetic — real photography + illustrated characters — becomes a core design direction for Drop 1 garments. The characters inhabit real Austin.
📱
For content too: Same concept works as Instagram content — characters appearing in real Austin spots. Shareable, distinctive, immediately recognizable as SBW.

Next step: When you're on a Rover walk or an Uber Eats run and you see a wall, a corner, a spot that feels right — photograph it with the character concept in mind. Ask: "would one of our characters belong here?" Build that reference library now. The character compositing happens in Adobe once the MacBook arrives.

Long-Term Creative Vision — Every 1–2 Years
THE RODEO CLOWN MUSIC VIDEOS

The concept: Cinematic short-form animated music videos featuring the three SBW rodeo clown characters. Released once a year or once every couple of years — labor intensive, high quality, potentially big-name collaborations. Each video is a standalone story but shares the same emotional core.

Episode 1 — The Full Story Arc
Act One — The Bottom

Opens in a dim apartment. Old takeout containers everywhere. Someone clearly hasn't left in days — heartbreak, or just been down and out so long they've stopped trying. The environment says everything without a word.

Slowly, "Tape Deck" by Jack Johnson starts to come on. Gentle. Hopeful. A little wistful. The three rodeo clown characters appear — not loudly, just there. Like they always were. They look at each other: "Okay. We're here to save this guy."

Act Two — The Rediscovery

They guide him through it. Not by fixing his problems — by introducing him to the creative parts of himself he'd forgotten. A pencil. A sketchbook. Music. Something made with his hands. Step by step he rediscovers himself. The apartment gets lighter as he starts making things again.

Act Three — The Turn

He's back. He's making things. He's alive again. And then — the moment the whole film has been building toward — he realizes something.

You can't keep what you don't give away.

The same principle that lives at the heart of AA's twelfth step. The same ancient wisdom that runs through every spiritual tradition that takes human transformation seriously: once you find happiness, you have to give it away in order to keep it. Holding it for yourself is the fastest way to lose it.

The final scene: he shows up at someone else's door. Maybe a friend he's been ignoring. Maybe a stranger whose light is clearly off. He knocks. The door opens. And quietly, the film ends — because the story doesn't. It's passing itself forward now.

The clowns watch from the end of the hallway. They nod at each other. Their work is done — because he's become one of them.

The Full Story Arc — Three Musical Movements
Movement 1 — "My Mind Is for Sale" — Jack Johnson

Opens on the bottom. The apartment. The clowns appear. This song plays as they help him through the painful recognition — the self-seeking, the fear, the selfishness that kept him stuck. The lyrics carry the confrontation. He begins to see himself clearly for the first time. The song is the mirror.

Movement 2 — "No Good With Faces" — Jack Johnson

The hard inner work. Steps 4–9. Writing with the sponsor. Going deep. The tempo and feel of "No Good With Faces" carries exactly the right weight — methodical, honest, moving through something difficult but necessary. Not dramatic, not easy. Just the work being done. The clowns present but quiet, letting him do it himself.

Movement 3 — "Upside Down" — Jack Johnson

The turn outward. The world that felt upside down is now right-side up — and the only way to keep it that way is to help someone else flip theirs. He starts helping others. Shows up at someone else's door. The clowns watch him go from the hallway and nod at each other. Their work is done because he's become one of them.

The Philosophical Foundation — For Internal Use

This film is rooted in AA's twelve steps — specifically the arc from self-recognition through the hard work of amends, to the twelfth step: carrying the message to others. You can't keep what you don't give away. That wisdom is ancient. It shows up in sobriety, in Buddhism, in every tradition that takes human transformation seriously.

This is never stated explicitly in the film. It doesn't need to be. People who have been through it will recognize it immediately. People who haven't will feel it without knowing why.

Internal Note — The Bigger Picture

As AI takes over more of the responsibilities that jobs require, people are going to start losing their sense of purpose and value. This is already happening and it will accelerate. When you get your time back — when the job that defined you no longer exists — you have to fill that space with something. Creativity is the perfect outlet for that. It gives you purpose in a world where the old sources of purpose are disappearing. SBW exists at exactly the right moment in history. These films are not just art. They are a response to something real that is happening to real people right now.

Why This Works

The rodeo clowns aren't saving someone from danger. They're saving someone from themselves. From stagnation. From the version of you that stopped making things and stopped trying. That's the entire SBW mission made visual — creative expression as the path back to yourself.
